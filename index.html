DEA
A sizable percentage of opioid overdose fatalities in recent years have been caused by the substance fentanyl. When the drug started to be combined with other narcotics like heroin and cocaine in the 2010s, it first attracted a lot of attention. The majority of the fentanyl that enters the United States, according to a report by the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA), is produced in China before being smuggled into the nation using a variety of methods, including shipping and mail services.

The drug has been linked to a considerable number of opioid overdose deaths in recent years, according to the research, which emphasizes the effect fentanyl has had on the opioid epidemic in the United States. Drug trafficking organizations find it to be lucrative due to its great potency and inexpensive production. Disrupting the supply chain and expanding addiction treatment and prevention initiatives have been the main goals of attempts to stop the flow of fentanyl into the country. Despite these initiatives, fentanyl continues to pose a serious problem for law enforcement and public health professionals.

Dangers
Overdose symptoms:
  • Slow, weak, or no breathing
  • Drowsiness / Dizziness
  • Nausea / Vomiting
  • Pinpoint pupils
  • Disorientation
  • Choking or gurgling
  • Limp body
  • Discolored skin (lips and nails)
  • Cold and clammy skin

Up to 100 times more strong than morphine, fentanyl is a potent synthetic opioid. Although it has the potential to be an effective painkiller for severe cases, it can be fatal in even modest dosages and is highly addictive. The risk of overdose is one of fentanyl's greatest risks. Even a small amount can be fatal, and determining the right dosage can be challenging. Furthermore, the likelihood of an overdose is increased since fentanyl is frequently combined, without the user's knowledge, with other narcotics like heroin or cocaine. Fentanyl use can potentially result in coma, death, and respiratory depression. As a result, it's critical to be aware of the risks associated with fentanyl and to get support if you're having addiction problems.
